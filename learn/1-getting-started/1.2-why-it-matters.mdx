# Why It Matters

Every thought you have ever written down, every photo you have ever uploaded, every message you have ever sent on social media, every trace of your digital life lives on a server owned by someone else. Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, or X (Twitter). If any of those companies shuts down, restructures, or simply decides your account violates a policy, your data goes with it. You do not own it. You cannot move it. And even when it is accessible, it exists at someone else's pleasure. They can pull the plug whenever they want.

:::note ⓘ Amnesiac Rental Space
The internet was never designed to preserve you. It was designed to serve pages. What we have today is an amnesiac rental space. You do not own your presence, you lease it from platforms that can evict you at any moment, or vanish entirely and take your history with them. When your data disappears, it takes your legacy with it, ***fading into oblivion as if you never existed.***
:::

Your digital life suffers from three compounding failures: it is fragmented beyond recovery, the companies holding the fragments can delete them at will, and the infrastructure storing them is concentrated in a handful of places that could be wiped off the map of Earth.

## Your digital life is scattered

Over the course of a lifetime, a person accumulates thousands of experiences worth preserving: lessons learned, stories lived, knowledge earned through decades of work and relationships. On today's internet, all of that ends up distributed across whichever platforms happened to be popular at the time. 

The photos from your twenties live in one cloud, the professional insights from your forties in another, and the messages where you told your children what mattered most to you sit locked inside a chat app that may not exist in ten years. None of these platforms share a common format, none of them let you search across the full breadth of what you have written, and none of them were designed to present your life as a coherent whole. 

What you know is spread so thin across so many services that even you cannot find it all, let alone pass it on.

## Deletion is inevitable

Even if you could identify all the scattered fragments, they would not be yours to keep. Every major platform reserves the right to delete your account, your content, and everything you have ever stored with them. Some do it after a period of inactivity. Others do it at their own discretion, with no appeal.

They have survived so far only by luck, but for how long.

## None of it will survive

Even the platforms that refrain from deleting your accounts cannot protect your data from where it physically lives. This vulnerability is not new, just the scale of it is.

When the Library of Alexandria burned, humanity lost irreplaceable works. But civilisation survived. Copies of those texts had already spread to libraries, monasteries, and private collections across the ancient world. The chain of inter-generational knowledge transfer weakened, but remained unbroken. Redundancy saved us.

Your digital life has no such redundancy. In <HDate g={2025} />, roughly 11,000 data centers hold virtually everything humanity has ever stored online ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-future-of-data-centers/)). The vast majority of that capacity is concentrated in just a few dozen metro areas, each one only half an hour away from the launch sites of Sarmat RS-28, Hwasong-19, Dongfeng-41, and Minuteman III.

<DataCenterMap />

The irony is that the backup of each cluster is another cluster, with its own GPS coordinates locked and loaded. That means humanity is half an hour away from entering a digital dark age. If a company does not delete your data, a disaster at these clusters will. When the single copy disappears, your legacy disappears with it.

The ancient world survived the loss of its greatest library because knowledge had been copied across the known world. The digital world has no equivalent. A century from now, your great-grandchildren will have no way to hear what you learned, what you believed, or what you wanted them to know. Not because the technology failed, but because nobody built the redundancy to make it last.
